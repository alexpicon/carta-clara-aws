[CHUNK eoir-01: What a Notice to Appear (NTA) is]
A Notice to Appear, Form I-862, is the charging document that the Department of
Homeland Security (DHS) uses to begin removal proceedings against a person in
immigration court. Removal proceedings are conducted under section 240 of the
Immigration and Nationality Act (INA). The NTA tells the person — called the
"respondent" — that the government believes they are removable from the United
States, lists the factual allegations and the legal charges against them, and orders
them to appear before an immigration judge. Receiving an NTA does not by itself mean a
person will be removed; it means a case has started and a judge will hear it.

[CHUNK eoir-02: What "removal proceedings" means in plain language]
"Removal proceedings" is the court process the U.S. government uses to decide whether
a person who is not a U.S. citizen can stay in the United States or must leave. The
proceedings take place in immigration court before an immigration judge, who is part
of the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) within the Department of
Justice. The proceedings are not a criminal case and immigration court is not a
criminal court. Being placed in removal proceedings is not a final decision — it is
the beginning of a process in which the respondent has the right to respond, to
present their case, and, in many situations, to ask the judge for relief that would
let them remain.

[CHUNK eoir-03: The information an NTA contains]
A Notice to Appear generally states: the nature of the proceedings; the legal
authority under which the proceedings are conducted; the factual allegations (often
numbered) describing what DHS says the respondent did or what their status is; the
charge(s) of removability, citing the specific section(s) of the INA; and the
requirement to appear before an immigration judge at a specified time and place. Some
NTAs list the hearing date, time, and court address; others state these will be set
later, and the court mails a separate hearing notice. A respondent should always
confirm the actual hearing date and location with the immigration court directly.

[CHUNK eoir-04: INA section 237(a)(1)(B) — remaining longer than permitted]
Section 237(a)(1)(B) of the Immigration and Nationality Act is a ground of
removability. In general terms, it provides that a noncitizen who was admitted to the
United States as a nonimmigrant (for example, on a visitor visa) and who has remained
in the United States for a time longer than permitted — commonly called an "overstay"
— is removable. A charge under INA 237(a)(1)(B) describes an alleged status violation;
it is an allegation that the immigration judge must consider, not a finding or a final
decision. What the allegation means for a particular person, and what defenses or
forms of relief may apply, are legal questions for a qualified attorney or accredited
representative.

[CHUNK eoir-05: The master calendar hearing]
The first hearing in removal proceedings is usually a "master calendar hearing." It is
a short, scheduling-focused hearing where the immigration judge handles preliminary
matters. At the master calendar hearing the judge typically confirms the respondent's
identity and address, advises the respondent of their rights, asks whether the
respondent has an attorney or needs time to find one, and addresses the allegations
and charges in the Notice to Appear. The judge may also set deadlines and schedule the
next hearing. Many respondents attend more than one master calendar hearing.

[CHUNK eoir-06: Pleading to the allegations and charges]
At a master calendar hearing, the immigration judge addresses the numbered factual
allegations and the charge(s) of removability in the Notice to Appear. The respondent,
usually through their attorney or accredited representative, responds to each
allegation and charge. The respondent may also state what relief from removal, if
any, they intend to seek. How to respond to allegations and charges is a legal
decision with significant consequences and should be made with a qualified attorney or
DOJ-accredited representative — it is not a decision to make alone or based on general
information.

[CHUNK eoir-07: The individual (merits) hearing]
If the case is contested or the respondent is applying for relief from removal, the
judge schedules an "individual hearing," also called a "merits hearing." The
individual hearing is longer than a master calendar hearing. At it, the respondent can
testify, present documents and witnesses, and make legal arguments, and DHS presents
its position. After considering the evidence, the immigration judge issues a decision.

[CHUNK eoir-08: The respondent's rights in immigration court]
A person in removal proceedings has important rights, including: the right to be
represented, at no expense to the government, by an attorney or a DOJ-accredited
representative of their choosing; the right to receive a list of free or low-cost
legal service providers; the right to an interpreter if they do not speak English
well; the right to examine the evidence against them; the right to present evidence,
documents, and witnesses on their own behalf; the right to a decision based on the
record; and, in general, the right to appeal an unfavorable decision to the Board of
Immigration Appeals. Exercising these rights effectively almost always requires the
help of a qualified legal representative.

[CHUNK eoir-09: Why attendance matters — failing to appear]
Hearing dates set by the immigration court are mandatory. If a respondent fails to
appear at a scheduled hearing, the immigration judge may proceed without them and may
order them removed "in absentia" (in their absence). An in-absentia removal order has
serious and lasting consequences. If a respondent has a genuine conflict, an
emergency, or a problem receiving notices, that is a situation to raise immediately
with a qualified legal representative and, through proper procedure, with the court.
Whether and how to attend, or to ask the court to change a date, is a legal and
procedural decision for a qualified attorney — not a decision to make based on general
information.

[CHUNK eoir-10: Getting a free or low-cost legal representative]
EOIR maintains a List of Pro Bono Legal Service Providers — organizations and
attorneys who may represent people in immigration court for free or at low cost. The
immigration court provides this list to respondents. Respondents are strongly
encouraged to seek a representative as early as possible, because preparing an
immigration case takes time. Nonprofit legal-aid organizations and DOJ-recognized
organizations are a safe place to start, and many offer a free initial consultation in
the respondent's own language.
