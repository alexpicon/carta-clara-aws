[CHUNK uscis-01: What the USCIS Avoid Scams program is]
USCIS wants to help you avoid becoming a victim of an immigration scam. Many people
offer help with immigration services. Unfortunately, not all are authorized to do so.
Although some may have good intentions, others are out to defraud you. The wrong help
can hurt your immigration case. It can cost you money, cause delays, or cause you to
lose your eligibility for an immigration benefit. Use the right immigration help so
your application is not delayed or denied.

[CHUNK uscis-02: The "notario público" warning]
In many Latin American countries, the term "notario público" (for "notary public")
stands for something very different than what it means in the United States. In many
Spanish-speaking nations, "notarios" are powerful attorneys with special legal
credentials. In the United States, however, notary publics are NOT authorized to
provide you with any legal services related to immigration. A notary public,
"notario," "notario público," or "immigration consultant" is not the same as an
attorney and cannot give you legal advice about your immigration case. Only an
attorney or an accredited representative working for a Department of Justice (DOJ)
recognized organization can give you legal advice about immigration matters.

[CHUNK uscis-03: Who is authorized to help you]
Two groups of people can give you legal advice and represent you before USCIS:
(1) Attorneys who are licensed and in good standing with a U.S. state bar and are not
under any court order restricting their practice of law; and (2) Accredited
representatives who work for a specific organization that has been recognized by the
Department of Justice. These accredited representatives can charge or accept only
small fees for their services. Notarios, notary publics, immigration consultants, and
businesses cannot give you legal advice unless they are also an authorized attorney
or accredited representative. To find authorized legal services, use the USCIS "Find
Legal Services" page or the list of recognized organizations and accredited
representatives published by the Department of Justice.

[CHUNK uscis-04: Common scam — people posing as USCIS officials]
Scammers may pose as USCIS officials. USCIS may contact you by telephone or email,
but USCIS officials will never ask you for payment over the phone or by email. If
USCIS needs payment, it will mail a letter on official stationery requesting payment.
Do not give payment over the phone to anyone who claims to be a USCIS official. If you
receive a scam email or phone call, report it to the Federal Trade Commission. If you
are not sure it is fraud, forward the suspicious email to the USCIS webmaster at
uscis.webmaster@uscis.dhs.gov. USCIS will review the emails received and share with
law enforcement agencies as appropriate.

[CHUNK uscis-05: Common scam — email and phishing schemes]
Scammers use email phishing schemes to obtain your personal information or money. A
scammer may send you an email that looks like it comes from USCIS, often copying the
USCIS logo and government-style language, and direct you to a fake website. The fake
site may ask you to enter personal information or pay a fee. USCIS will not ask you to
pay fees to a person on the phone or by email. You can pay some fees online through
your USCIS online account at my.uscis.gov, but always type the address yourself
rather than clicking a link in an email. Make sure any website address ends in ".gov".

[CHUNK uscis-06: Common scam — fake or unofficial websites]
Some websites claiming to be affiliated with USCIS offer step-by-step guidance on
completing a USCIS application or petition, and may charge a fee for blank government
forms or for information that USCIS provides for free. The official USCIS website is
uscis.gov, and all USCIS forms are free to download at uscis.gov/forms. You do not
have to pay to download or file most forms beyond the official filing fee. If a
website asks you to pay for a blank form, it is not an official government site.

[CHUNK uscis-07: Common scam — payment by unusual methods]
Be very cautious if anyone asks you to pay for immigration help using a gift card,
prepaid debit card, wire transfer, payment app, or cryptocurrency. The U.S. government
does not accept these payment methods for immigration fees. A demand for payment by
gift card or wire transfer is one of the clearest signs of a scam.

[CHUNK uscis-08: Common scam — "guaranteed" results and the visa lottery]
Be cautious of anyone who guarantees results or faster processing in exchange for a
fee. No one can guarantee that USCIS will approve your application. Only USCIS or an
immigration judge can decide your case. Also be aware of scams related to the
Diversity Visa (DV) "green card lottery": the U.S. Department of State runs the DV
program for free, the only official website to enter is the State Department site,
and entrants are notified only through the official Entrant Status Check — not by
email or letter demanding a fee.

[CHUNK uscis-09: Tips to protect yourself]
Protect yourself: Do not sign any blank forms or documents. Do not sign any forms or
documents that contain false information or that you do not understand. Always ask for
and keep copies of every document and form prepared or filed for you. Never let anyone
keep your original personal documents, such as your passport or birth certificate.
Get a written contract, in a language you understand, that lists all services and
fees. Make sure your application lists your own contact information, not the
preparer's, so USCIS notices come directly to you. If a form preparer helped you, the
preparer must list their name and information on the form. Walk away from anyone who
pressures you to act immediately or who tells you not to read what you are signing.

[CHUNK uscis-10: How to report an immigration scam]
If you believe you have been the victim of an immigration services scam, report it.
You can report scams to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at ReportFraud.ftc.gov or
by calling 1-877-FTC-HELP (1-877-382-4357). Many states also accept complaints about
the unauthorized practice of immigration law through their state attorney general or
consumer protection office. To report a website or social media account that falsely
claims to be affiliated with USCIS, email uscis.webmaster@uscis.dhs.gov. Reporting a
scam — even if you did not lose money — helps protect others in your community.
