[CHUNK ftc-01: Why immigration scams are dangerous]
Getting help with an immigration application can make the process easier. But some
people and businesses that promise help are really after your money. For many people,
their immigration status can be put at risk by filling out the wrong form, missing a
deadline, or putting incorrect information on a form. Scammers take advantage of that
fear. Knowing how to spot a scam — and where to get real help — protects your money
and your case.

[CHUNK ftc-02: Notarios are not lawyers in the United States]
In many Latin American countries, a "notario público" is a highly trained, specially
licensed lawyer. In the United States, that is not what a notary public is. In the
U.S., notarios and notary publics are NOT lawyers. They are not authorized to give
you legal advice or to provide immigration services, and you should not pay them a
fee to do so. Time after time, notarios take people's money and documents, make big
promises, and then do nothing — often hurting your chances of immigrating lawfully
while they disappear with your papers and your cash.

[CHUNK ftc-03: Who can legally give you immigration help]
Only two kinds of people can legally give you advice about your immigration case and
represent you: (1) an attorney licensed to practice law in the United States, and
(2) an accredited representative who works for an organization recognized by the
Department of Justice (DOJ). DOJ-recognized organizations often provide help for free
or for a low cost. You can search the DOJ list of recognized organizations and
accredited representatives, or use a nonprofit legal-aid clinic, to confirm that the
person helping you is authorized.

[CHUNK ftc-04: Red flag — guarantees and big promises]
No one can guarantee that your immigration application will be approved. Only the U.S.
government can decide your case. If someone promises a guaranteed result, a special
relationship with the government, a quick green card, or that they "know someone" who
can make your case succeed, that is a red flag for a scam. Honest, authorized help
will explain the process and the risks — it will not promise an outcome.

[CHUNK ftc-05: Red flag — blank forms and false information]
Do not sign blank immigration forms. Do not sign forms that contain false information
about you, and do not sign anything you do not understand. A dishonest notario,
accountant, or consultant may ask you to do this. False information on an immigration
form can cause your application to be denied and can put your immigration status at
risk. Always read — or have someone you trust read to you — every form before you
sign it, and keep a copy of everything you sign.

[CHUNK ftc-06: Red flag — paying for free forms and free information]
You should not have to pay anyone for blank immigration forms. Official U.S.
government immigration forms are free to download from the official USCIS website,
uscis.gov. Real, reliable immigration information is available for free from the
government. If a person or website charges you a fee just to give you a blank form or
basic information, that is a warning sign.

[CHUNK ftc-07: Red flag — payment by gift card, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency]
If someone says you must pay them by gift card, prepaid debit card, wire transfer,
payment app, or cryptocurrency to help with your immigration status, that is a scam.
The U.S. government does not demand payment that way. Scammers like these payment
methods because the money is hard to trace and hard to get back. A demand to pay this
way — especially urgently — means you should stop and walk away.

[CHUNK ftc-08: Red flag — fake government calls, emails, and websites]
Scammers pretend to be from the government. They may call, email, or text and claim
to be from USCIS, ICE, or "the immigration office," and then demand money or personal
information, sometimes threatening arrest or deportation if you do not pay
immediately. The real U.S. government will not call to demand immediate payment or
threaten you. To recognize a real U.S. government website, check the web address: if
it ends in ".gov", it is an official U.S. government site. Do not click links in
unexpected emails or texts — type the official address yourself.

[CHUNK ftc-09: Red flag — pressure, urgency, and keeping your documents]
Be cautious of anyone who pressures you to decide or pay right now, who tells you not
to read what you are signing, or who wants to keep your original documents such as
your passport, birth certificate, or other identity papers. A trustworthy provider
gives you time, answers your questions, gives you a written contract in a language you
understand, gives you receipts, and returns your original documents to you.

[CHUNK ftc-10: How to get real help and how to report a scam]
To get real help, look for a licensed attorney or a DOJ-accredited representative,
including nonprofit legal-aid organizations that serve immigrants for free or low
cost. Before you pay anyone, confirm they are authorized. If you or someone you know
has seen or experienced an immigration-related scam, report it to the Federal Trade
Commission at ReportFraud.ftc.gov. Reporting helps the FTC and law enforcement stop
scammers and protect other families — and you can report even if you did not lose
money and even if you are not asked for your immigration status.
